## Site structure

Keep the top-level nav to five items and nest everything else underneath as subpages. This gives visitors a clear hierarchy so they instantly understand who the firm is, what it offers, and what to do next — and it helps search engines index the site properly too.Two things sit outside that tree and stay pinned in the header on every single page, not buried in a menu: a **client portal login** and a **"Book a Consultation" button**. Clients should never have to email your firm asking where to send documents — leading CPA sites solve this with a persistent portal link in the top-right corner of the nav, pointing to a dedicated tool like SmartVault, Liscio, ShareFile, TaxDome, or Canopy.

## Page-by-page breakdown

**Homepage**
- One clear sentence above the fold naming who they serve and what makes them different. Firms that name a specific audience — "CPA for VC-backed startups" instead of "accounting services for individuals and businesses" — convert better because the visitor instantly recognizes the firm understands their situation.
- A visible "book a consultation" button above the fold plus click-to-call on mobile — this is considered non-negotiable functionality on an accounting site today, along with services named in plain language rather than vague icons.
- A trust strip: license/credential badges, years in business, or a star rating pulled from Google.
- One strong real photo of the CPA or team rather than stock imagery — firms that invest in a genuine photograph consistently read as more credible than ones leaning on generic stock.

**About**
- Founder/team bios with real photos. Named team members with real photos and credentials matter more in accounting than almost any other industry, since the visitor is being asked to trust this person with their financial data.
- Credentials spelled out: CPA license number and state, years of experience, specializations, professional memberships (AICPA, state CPA society).
- Firm story — personality differentiates surprisingly well here. Several standout firm sites skip corporate accounting-speak entirely and write service descriptions as a direct, personal conversation with the prospect.

**Services**
- Individual sections per offering: tax planning & prep, bookkeeping, payroll, audit & assurance, business advisory/CFO services, entity formation, IRS problem resolution.
- Niche pages if the client has a specialization. The firms winning organic search today generally aren't ranking for a broad term like "CPA near me" — they're ranking for specific niches like "real estate CPA" or "dental practice CPA." A dedicated page per niche lets a firm own long-tail searches that a generalist competes for against hundreds of local results.
- Pricing: at least show tiered ranges, or a simple calculator. Most of the highest-performing accounting sites publish some form of pricing, because prospects comparing firms across open browser tabs tend to skip any firm that forces a call just to learn the cost.

**Resources / Blog**
- Tax deadline calendar, simple calculators, downloadable checklists (good for email capture).
- Blog posts targeting specific long-tail questions — this becomes the SEO engine long after launch.
- FAQ: fees, what to bring to a first meeting, how the engagement actually works.

**Contact**
- Form, phone, embedded Google Map, office hours. The best-performing sites offer phone, form, chat, and booking simultaneously — different visitors have different comfort levels, and more entry points capture more leads overall.

## Trust signals that matter specifically here

Generic "we're trustworthy" copy doesn't do much in this industry. The strongest CPA sites make trust explicit rather than implied: the state board license number in the footer, AICPA and state-society membership badges above the fold, and the preparer's IRS PTIN on bio pages. Prospects scan for exactly these signals within seconds, so leaving them unstated is functionally the same as not having them. A few real examples done well: Dark Horse CPAs puts hard numbers front and center (an 80% NPS score, 550+ five-star reviews, a 90% retention rate) inside expandable cards; MyBookly embeds named Google reviews with detailed responses directly on its homepage; The Allen CPA Firm gives its free-consultation offer a dedicated nav page and pairs professional-association logos with testimonials that speak specifically to responsiveness.

## Design and visual identity

The homepage examples pulled up above give a sense of the range, but a few patterns repeat across almost every well-regarded firm site:
- **Color:** navy, deep green, and gold or warm-neutral accents dominate — colors associated with stability and trust. One standout small-firm site pairs a single low-angle architectural photo with restrained gold accents and reads as tier-one financial-consultancy quality at a small-firm price point.
- **Photography:** real photos over stock or clipart. The common thread across the best examples is investing in one or two genuine photographs and cutting back hard on stock imagery — a clipart calculator graphic or a generic city-skyline handshake photo are the two visual clichés worth avoiding entirely.
- **Bilingual toggle:** if the client serves a bilingual community, an EN/ES toggle is a genuine trust differentiator, not just a nice-to-have.

## Security and legal — the part your student projects never had to cover

This is the biggest real gap between what you've built before and what this needs.

**Data handling.** CPAs handle SSNs, bank statements, and tax returns. Sending that over plain email actually violates the Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act, which is why a genuine secure-upload system needs to replace email attachments entirely — this is what the client portal solves. Non-negotiable regardless of budget: SSL site-wide, a real privacy policy, and two-factor authentication on any login you build yourself.

**Accessibility (ADA/WCAG).** This one surprises almost everyone building their first US business site. A small number of law firms file the large majority of website-accessibility lawsuits as high-volume operations, and most start as a demand letter — often for $10,000-$25,000 — rather than an actual court filing. 77% of these lawsuits target companies under $25 million in revenue, and professional services firms are a named target category, not just e-commerce. Installing an accessibility-overlay widget alone doesn't reliably fix this — more than 22% of 2025 lawsuits targeted sites that already had one installed. Building it in properly matters more: real alt text, full keyboard navigation, sufficient color contrast, labeled form fields, captioned video. Conformance with WCAG 2.1 AA significantly reduces the risk, and a conformant site backed by documentation from a real audit gives you something solid to point to if a letter ever does arrive.

## What $10,000 should actually buy

At this budget, the client should expect fully custom design rather than a reskinned template, somewhere around 10-15 pages, real functionality beyond a basic brochure site, an SEO foundation, and professional-quality copywriting — you're being paid for strategic planning as much as visual design. For context: Squarespace sites with no custom development typically run under $5,000, custom-designed WordPress builds fall in the $5,000-$15,000 range, and only venture-backed firms with dedicated marketing teams spend $50,000+. $10K sits comfortably in "fully custom, properly built" territory, which is exactly what everything above assumes.

A rough way to allocate your own effort: roughly a third goes to design/UX and content structure, the majority goes to development — the portal integration, forms, booking, and SEO setup are where the real time goes, not the visual layer.

## Before you write a line of code: get this from your client

- Every service they actually offer, in their own words — don't assume; some CPAs do estate work, some don't, some specialize by industry
- Their CPA license number and state, PTIN if applicable, and professional memberships
- Real headshots — push back gently on stock photos, since this is one of the biggest credibility levers in this specific industry
- Existing branding (logo, colors), or a green light for you to help establish simple brand guidelines
- Whichever client-portal or practice-management tool they already use or want — you're usually integrating a login, not building document storage from scratch
- 3-5 real testimonials, or permission to embed their Google reviews
- One sentence answering "who is your ideal client" — this single answer shapes almost every copy decision above
